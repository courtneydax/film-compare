Film Stock Reference

what to expect from each stock, and better prompts to test ← Back to Film Compare
Two easy sanity-check stocks: Cinestill 800T's red halation glow around highlights should be either clearly present or clearly absent, and Velvia 50 should look aggressively oversaturated next to everything else. If a model doesn't nail those two, it's probably not doing stock-specific rendering — just adding generic "film grain."

T2I / I2I prompt template

Same trait descriptor either way — just swap the lead-in verb depending on whether you're generating fresh or editing an existing photo.

T2I: Shot on [Stock], 35mm film, [traits]
I2I: Convert this photo to [Stock], 35mm film, [traits] (or Apply film emulation:)

Model notes

Based on vendor docs / community reports, treat as a starting point to verify empirically — entries with a "Hands-on so far" note have been checked against real output in this tool.

T2I

Krea 2 Turbo
Strong at grain, motion blur, low-dynamic-range "captured" look. Has a creativity/raw dial that controls literal prompt adherence — set it to raw, or it aesthetically reinterprets your stock prompt instead of rendering it literally.

Hands-on so far: on the Cinestill 800T neon-night showcase prompt, it hallucinated garbled, non-real text on several of the background signs — worth a close look on any prompt with text-bearing signage in frame.

Garbled sign text
Chroma
FLUX-based, trained on a broader/less-curated dataset spanning artistic, photographic, and niche styles. Likely more headroom for gritty/analog looks than heavily-curated commercial models — not directly benchmarked for film stock specifically.

Hands-on so far: reliably rendered the "Portra 800 pushed to 1600" concert scene in black and white regardless of prompting, so that showcase example used Grok Imagine instead. Also underdelivered on four of the more diagnostic traits — barely any halation around Cinestill 800T's highlights, Velvia 50 came out closer to a naturalistic sunset than the aggressively oversaturated look the stock is known for, Portra 400 rendered with punchier contrast/detail than the muted, soft, low-contrast look that's the stock's whole reputation, and the Portra 800 dinner scene left both faces essentially in silhouette — defeating the point of a skin-tone test.

Weak halation
Undersaturated
Too contrasty
No visible skin tones
Seedream
Responds well to photographic language (film stock, lens, lighting). Avoid stacking style adjectives ("cinematic, photorealistic, professional") — use concrete nouns and one style cue instead to avoid muddy output.

Hands-on so far: the only model tried that clearly nailed Cinestill 800T's signature red/pink halation bloom around streetlamps, plus the cool teal shadow cast — the showcase example now uses this instead of Chroma/Grok/Krea attempts that all missed the halation. A first attempt at Fujicolor Superia X-tra 400 came out too muted and low-contrast, but a second attempt came very close to matching Grok's punch and saturation — still with noticeably heavier, more visible grain throughout.

Clear halation
Close, heavier grain
Qwen Image
Solid general prompt adherence. Reported as slower/more deliberate — better suited to a finishing pass than bulk iteration.
Grok Imagine
Has a distinct house style historically harder to steer with fine-grained aesthetic prompts. No direct film-stock benchmark found.

Hands-on so far: solid all-around performer — current showcase examples for Kodak Gold 200, Portra 400, Portra 800, Portra 800 pushed to 1600, and Velvia 50 all use it. Its Cinestill 800T attempt had good mood and color grading but, like Chroma and Krea, didn't clearly render the defining halation bloom — Seedream's attempt was more accurate on that specific trait.

Good mood, weak halation
Z-Image Turbo
Fast/distilled. Adherence is generally solid, but turbo/distilled models often trade away fine stylistic nuance for speed.

I2I

FireRed (FireRed-Image-Edit-1.0)
Open-source. Training data explicitly separates Semantic / Stylistic / Structural editing, and old-photo restoration is a named capability — good sign for film-emulation edits specifically.
Qwen-Image-Edit
Preserves identity, lighting, and color tone across edits. Rewards patience over speed, similar to its T2I sibling.
Grok
No direct data found on I2I film-stock handling.
Seedream v5
Same photographic-language strength as the T2I model — likely carries over to edits, though not separately confirmed.
